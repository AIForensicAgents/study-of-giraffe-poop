# Study of Giraffe Poop
A light-hearted but rigorous repository for educational and field-study resources on giraffe scat (droppings, feces). Intended for ecologists, wildlife educators, students, and curious naturalists who want to learn how giraffe fecal observations can inform ecology, behavior, and conservation.

---

## Overview
This repository collects notes, photographic examples, observational metadata guidelines, and high-level best practices for documenting giraffe feces in the field. It is NOT a lab protocol repository — it focuses on non-invasive, observational, and ethical field-study practices and on using fecal evidence as part of broader ecological inference.

Audience:
- Field ecologists and students learning scat-based inference
- Citizen scientists and wildlife guides
- Educators preparing natural history materials
- Conservation practitioners monitoring diet and habitat use

What you'll find here:
- Practical morphology notes to help with in-situ description and categorization
- Guidance on habitat- and diet-related indicators visible in feces
- Ethics & safety considerations for fieldwork and specimen handling
- A curated image gallery with representative photos (see below)
- Ideas for future analyses and study directions

---

## Why it matters
Giraffe feces are a surprisingly rich source of ecological information. Systematic, non-invasive observations can contribute to:
- Diet assessment: presence of browse items, seeds, or specific plant fragments
- Habitat use inference: linking droppings with vegetation communities and water sources
- Nutritional and health indicators: consistency and content can flag seasonal shifts in forage quality or animal condition
- Ecosystem services tracking: scat contributes nutrients and seeds to the environment, influencing plant regeneration and soil biota
- Conservation monitoring: non-invasive sampling complements telemetry and observational surveys for population and behavior studies

Observational scat data are low-cost, scalable, and suitable for citizen science, making them valuable for long-term monitoring programs.

## Morphology Notes
Use standardized descriptors when recording observations. Below are practical fields and what to look for:

- Shape and size
  - Typical giraffe scat: rounded/ovoid pellets aggregated in piles. Note pellet diameter (cm) and approximate mass (qualitative: small/medium/large).
- Color and consistency
  - Range: dark brown to olive green to tan depending on diet and age of material.
  - Consistency descriptors: firm, friable, soft, mucilaginous.
- Surface texture
  - Smooth, fibrous, or containing visible leaf/branch fragments.
- Contents (visible without lab processing)
  - Bark, leaves, twigs, seeds, hairs, insect fragments.
- Odor (describe qualitatively)
  - Freshness indicator; strong ammonia-like smell suggests recent deposition.
- Age/Weathering
  - Fresh, partially weathered, or desiccated — affects color and texture.
- Contextual notes
  - Number of pellets per pile, spacing, association with tracks, and if the animal was observed nearby.

Record metadata: GPS coordinates, time/date, observer, weather, associated vegetation, and animals seen/heard.

## Habitat / Diet Clues
What fecal observations can tell you about giraffe foraging and habitat:

- Browse vs. graze
  - High proportion of twig and leaf fragments indicates browsing (expected for giraffes). Presence of grass fragments could indicate opportunistic feeding or contamination.
- Plant species indicators
  - Intact seeds or characteristic leaf fragments can help identify consumed plant taxa (when combined with local flora knowledge).
- Seasonal diet shifts
  - Color and fiber content often vary seasonally; greener, softer droppings may indicate fresh leaf consumption during rainy seasons.
- Foraging height and patch use
  - Pellet piles beneath particular tree species or in specific stand types can reveal preferred feeding patches.
- Nutritional condition and stress
  - Consistently watery or unusually scarce droppings across individuals may warrant further health assessment (subject to veterinary/biological follow-up).
- Ecosystem interactions
  - Scat with many seeds suggests potential role in seed dispersal; abundant insect fragments may indicate incidental consumption or habitat overlap.

Use fecal indicators in combination with direct observation, vegetation surveys, and remote-sensing/GIS for robust inference.

## Ethics / Safety
Safety and ethics are paramount. Follow these principles:

- Permits & permissions
  - Obtain research permits and landowner permissions before systematic sampling or photography on private or protected lands.
- Minimize disturbance
  - Do not pursue animals to obtain fresh samples or photographs. Maintain a respectful distance; use optics or long lenses.
- Personal protective measures
  - Wear gloves when handling fresh material. Avoid touching your face; wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork. Carry basic first-aid supplies.
- Sample handling (high-level)
  - If collecting samples for lab work, follow institutional biosafety guidance and chain-of-custody procedures. Store and transport samples according to regulations.
- Legal & ethical constraints
  - Do not remove or collect material from areas where it is prohibited. Respect local wildlife laws and cultural considerations.
- Data sharing & privacy
  - Be mindful when sharing GPS locations of endangered species; consider obfuscating exact coordinates in public datasets to reduce poaching or disturbance risk.

If in doubt, consult local wildlife authorities or institutional biosafety officers.

## Future Work
Planned directions and opportunities for collaboration:

- Standardized metadata templates
  - Publish a minimal observation schema (CSV/JSON) for easy aggregation and citizen-science integration.
- Image classification
  - Build labeled datasets for automated recognition of freshness, pellet size classes, and visible diet items.
- Non-invasive molecular follow-up
  - Coordinate with labs to design ethical, permit-compliant metabarcoding projects to identify plant taxa from fecal DNA.
- Long-term monitoring pilots
  - Establish transects and repeat sampling to quantify seasonal diet shifts and habitat use.
- Educational outreach
  - Develop field guides and citizen science modules to train park staff, guides, and volunteers in harmless observation techniques.
- Interdisciplinary studies
  - Link fecal observations with remote sensing, camera traps, and vegetation surveys to produce integrative habitat-use models.

Contributions welcome! See CONTRIBUTING.md for suggested steps (if present) or open an issue to discuss ideas.
